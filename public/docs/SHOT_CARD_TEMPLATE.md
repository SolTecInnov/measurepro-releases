# Shot Card Template & Usage Guide

## What is a Shot Card?

A **shot card** (also called a shot list card or camera card) is a physical or digital checklist card used during film production. It contains all the critical information needed to capture a specific shot, ensuring nothing is missed and maintaining consistency across the production.

Think of it as a **recipe card for filming** - it tells the camera operator, director, and crew exactly what ingredients (equipment), techniques (camera settings), and steps (setup requirements) are needed to achieve the desired shot.

---

## Why Use Shot Cards?

### Benefits:
✅ **Prevents missed shots** - Systematic checklist ensures complete coverage  
✅ **Saves time** - Crew knows exactly what's needed before arriving on location  
✅ **Maintains consistency** - Same settings/style across multiple shooting days  
✅ **Enables delegation** - Different crew members can shoot from same plan  
✅ **Tracks progress** - Easy to see what's captured, what's pending  
✅ **Budget control** - Know exactly what equipment to rent for each scene

## How to Use Shot Cards on Set

### Before Shooting Day:

1. **Create cards** for all shots in your scene
2. **Print or load digitally** (tablets work well)
3. **Organize by scene** and shooting order
4. **Distribute to crew** - everyone gets relevant cards

### On Shooting Day:

1. **Review card** before setting up
2. **Gather equipment** listed on card
3. **Set up camera** per card specifications
4. **Frame and light** according to notes
5. **Rehearse** if talent involved
6. **Capture shot**
7. **Check box** "Shot Captured"
8. **Review footage** - check box "Approved" or "Needs Reshoot"

### After Shooting:

1. **Organize captured shots** by status
2. **Flag reshoots** immediately
3. **Archive cards** with footage for reference
4. **Note any changes** made on-set (for future reference)

## Digital vs. Physical Cards

### Physical Cards (Printed)
**Pros:**
- No battery/screen required
- Easy to mark up and annotate
- Can pass around crew quickly
- Weatherproof if laminated

**Cons:**
- Wasteful if changes happen
- Harder to update in real-time
- Can get lost/damaged

### Digital Cards (iPad/Tablet)
**Pros:**
- Easy to update on-the-fly
- Searchable and filterable
- Can sync across crew devices
- Environmentally friendly

**Cons:**
- Requires charged devices
- Screen glare in bright sun
- Need app or software

**Recommended Apps:**
- **Shotlister** (industry standard)
- **Shot Lister Pro**
- **Google Sheets** (free, customizable)
- **Notion** (flexible, collaborative)

## Customizing for Your Production

Feel free to modify the template to fit your needs:

**Add fields for:**
- Storyboard thumbnail
- Reference images
- VFX/CGI notes
- Color grading notes
- Estimated time per shot
- Cost/budget tracking

**Remove fields if:**
- Not using professional lighting (natural light only)
- Solo shooter (no crew coordination needed)
- Guerrilla-style filming (minimal planning)

## Quick Reference: Shot Sizes

- **Extreme Wide (EWS):** Entire scene, establishing
- **Wide (WS):** Full subject in environment
- **Medium Wide (MWS):** Subject + some environment
- **Medium (MS):** Waist up
- **Medium Close-Up (MCU):** Chest up
- **Close-Up (CU):** Face/detail
- **Extreme Close-Up (ECU):** Eyes, hands, macro

## Quick Reference: Camera Angles

- **Eye Level:** Natural, neutral
- **High Angle:** Looking down (subject appears smaller)
- **Low Angle:** Looking up (subject appears larger/powerful)
- **Dutch/Canted:** Tilted (unease, tension)
- **Overhead/Bird's Eye:** Straight down (90°)
