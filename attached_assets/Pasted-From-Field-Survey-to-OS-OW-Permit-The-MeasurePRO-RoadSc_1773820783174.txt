From Field Survey to OS/OW Permit: The MeasurePRO + RoadScope Workflow
Oversize and overweight permits depend on one critical ingredient: trustworthy field data. Without a repeatable process from survey to analysis, you risk unexpected low structures, denied permits, or last‑minute reroutes.
MeasurePRO and RoadScope form a complete workflow that takes you from LiDAR‑based field surveys to corridor‑wide clearance analysis and permit‑ready documentation.
1. Planning the survey route
Everything starts before the truck leaves the yard:
•	Identify the candidate permitted route and any known high‑risk segments (older bridges, urban corridors, complex interchanges).
•	Define the planned load envelope (height, width, length) so you know what clearances you need to validate.
•	In MeasurePRO, create a new survey project and give it a clear name that matches the permit application or move ID.
Having a named project and documented route plan makes it easy to tie field data to specific moves later.
2. Configuring MeasurePRO and vehicle profiles
Before driving, MeasurePRO is configured around the vehicle and load:
•	Select or create a vehicle profile that matches the planned OS/OW configuration (tractor, trailer type, axle counts, overall height).
•	Confirm LiDAR and GPS/RTK connections and check fix quality and satellite count.
•	Choose logging behavior: continuous logging along the corridor plus explicit POIs for bridges, signals, power lines, and other overhead features.
•	Set warning and critical clearance thresholds based on the load’s envelope.
This configuration ensures that every measurement is interpreted in the context of the vehicle actually doing the move.
3. Running the field survey with MeasurePRO
On the road, MeasurePRO turns the survey vehicle into a rolling sensor suite:
•	The driver or surveyor follows the planned route while MeasurePRO logs LiDAR measurements, GPS positions, speed, and time.
•	Voice commands trigger POIs and control logging without taking hands off the wheel.
•	Each structure of interest (bridges, overhead utilities, gantries, tunnels) is captured with measured clearance and tagged with a POI type.
•	Photos and video provide visual confirmation and context for later review.
By the time the vehicle returns to the yard, you have a detailed trace of the route with clearances and obstacles recorded in real time.
4. Exporting survey data from MeasurePRO
After the run, the survey project is exported from MeasurePRO in standard engineering and GIS formats:
•	CSV for tabular review, quick sanity checks, and sharing via spreadsheets.
•	GeoJSON or Shapefile for GIS tools and mapping platforms.
•	LandXML and DXF for AutoCAD Civil 3D and design workflows.
Each export includes coordinates, POI types, measured clearances, timestamps, and any operator notes, forming a complete field data record for that route.
5. Importing the survey into RoadScope
RoadScope consumes the exported data to give you a corridor‑wide view:
•	Import the MeasurePRO export file into RoadScope and associate it with the corresponding route or permit request.
•	RoadScope maps all measured points, color‑coding them by clearance status (safe, warning, critical).
•	You can overlay other data layers such as known restrictions, previous surveys, and customer constraints.
This step turns a collection of measurements into a clear visual of where the route is strong and where it’s at risk.
6. Performing clearance analysis and route decisions
With the survey visualized in RoadScope, the engineering and permitting team can:
•	Identify all structures that fall near or below your warning and critical thresholds.
•	Compare alternate alignments or bypasses, using historical surveys where available.
•	Document why a route is acceptable, requires mitigation (e.g., lane shifts, bucket truck support), or should be rejected.
Because the analysis is built on field data, you can justify route choices to customers, regulators, and internal safety teams.
7. Generating permit‑ready outputs
Once the route decision is made, the same MeasurePRO + RoadScope data can feed your permit package:
•	Generate bridge and overhead clearance tables that reference measured values and coordinates.
•	Export maps showing measured points, risk zones, and any required mitigations.
•	Attach photos or video clips for particularly tight locations as visual evidence.
Many carriers and engineering firms standardize these outputs so each permit application follows a consistent, repeatable format.
8. Example: Single corridor, multiple moves
One of the biggest benefits of this workflow is reusability:
•	A corridor surveyed once with MeasurePRO can be reused for multiple similar loads, as long as equipment and envelope remain within known limits.
•	Additional surveys can be layered over time to reflect construction changes or new restrictions.
•	RoadScope keeps these surveys organized so planners can quickly answer “Have we run this corridor before with a similar load?”
Over time, your organization builds a growing database of trusted routes instead of treating each move as a blank slate.
9. Why standardizing this workflow matters
By standardizing on MeasurePRO in the field and RoadScope in the office, you:
•	Reduce the chance of surprises on move day.
•	Shorten the time from “we might take this route” to an evidence‑backed “yes” or “no.”
•	Provide regulators and customers with a clear record of how clearances were checked.
•	Build an internal knowledge base of safe, proven OS/OW corridors.
That’s the difference between treating each oversize move as a one‑off project and building a scalable, auditable OS/OW operation
