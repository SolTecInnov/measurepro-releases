It sounds good — but this summary is exactly the kind of “demo-level” write-up that can hide major cracks. I don’t care that it “works on their machine.” I care that it’s correct, robust, and defendable for 100–300 km routes and real field chaos.

Here’s what I think: 80% promising, 20% red flags. You need to verify a few non-negotiables before you trust it.

⸻

The good (if true)
	•	Split view + bidirectional sync is the right architecture.
	•	Offline-first IndexedDB is correct for MeasurePRO.
	•	ZIP bundle export is exactly what you need for portability and audit.
	•	Creating alignments from driven paths + simplification is practical.

⸻

The red flags / what’s likely wrong

1) “Snap to nearest sample point” can be a mistake

They changed profile→map sync to snap to nearest sample instead of using stationing continuously. That can be fine only if your sampling is dense enough and uniform.

If your samples are sparse (or you filter points), snapping causes:
	•	the marker to jump
	•	station to drift
	•	mismatch between alignment geometry and displayed station

Better approach: snap to station on alignment (continuous) and interpolate profile value at station (linear / spline).
Snapping to samples is a workaround for a deeper mapping issue.

✅ Acceptable if they ALSO show “sample spacing” and allow interpolation.
❌ Bad if it’s the only mode.

2) “Elevation” wording — they say elevation again

They wrote “elevation profile graph” and “CSV with station, elevation.”
You explicitly standardized on Altitude (MSL) and corrected altitude.

This isn’t just naming. It’s semantics:
	•	raw altitude vs corrected altitude
	•	MSL vs ellipsoid
	•	offset applied

The export must include:
	•	station (s)
	•	raw altitude candidates (if stored)
	•	selected/derived altitude
	•	corrected altitude
	•	altitude strategy + offset metadata

If they export “elevation” as a single number with no provenance, it’s not audit-proof.

3) Alignment-from-driving-path can create garbage geometry

Douglas–Peucker simplification is fine, but it can:
	•	cut corners on curves
	•	break stationing accuracy
	•	create false “shortcuts” at intersections/loops
	•	make projection to alignment wrong

You need:
	•	constraints (max lateral deviation)
	•	handling of loops / backtracking
	•	a way to edit alignment manually or re-fit

If they didn’t address loops, your stationing becomes nonsense.

4) They didn’t mention “lateral offset” or side-of-road sign

For convoys, you need:
	•	lateral offset from alignment
	•	sign (right positive)
	•	heading delta (vehicle vs alignment tangent)

Without offset, “alignment” is just a pretty line. It doesn’t quantify clearance corridor positioning.

5) No mention of projection math quality

You need to know:
	•	do they project point-to-polyline in meters with proper geo conversion?
	•	are they using a correct local planar projection (e.g., Web Mercator approximations are okay locally, but can drift)?
	•	do they precompute cumulative distances correctly?

If their projection is sloppy, stationing errors accumulate and the linkage becomes “feels right” but wrong.

⸻

The 10 checks you must demand (no excuses)

Tell them to demonstrate these, with numbers on screen:
	1.	Station correctness: pick 3 known points; station increases smoothly; no jumps.
	2.	Interpolation: drag cursor between samples — does the marker move smoothly or jump?
	3.	Sample spacing display: show average distance between samples.
	4.	Loop/backtrack handling: drive a loop; does alignment creation produce sane stationing?
	5.	Lateral offset: show offset (m) from alignment for the live point.
	6.	Heading delta: show difference between alignment bearing and GNSS heading.
	7.	Long distance: load 200–300 km alignment/profile; no lag, no NaN, usable axis.
	8.	Export audit: ZIP includes metadata.json with:
	•	altitude strategy
	•	offset
	•	axis mapping id/confidence (if relevant)
	•	timestamps
	9.	Re-import: can you import the ZIP and reproduce same stationing?
	10.	Offline boot: airplane mode; fresh reload; can you still load/switch alignments/profiles?

If they can’t demo these, it’s not “complete,” it’s a prototype.
